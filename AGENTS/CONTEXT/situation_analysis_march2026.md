## What I Think You Should Do

I'm not a financial advisor or career coach, and you know your life better than I do. But since you asked:

### 1. Accept the unemployment timeline and plan for it

Swiss ALV (Arbeitslosenversicherung) is one of the best safety nets in the world. If you qualify — and with your recent consulting projects through BigBear Engineering, you likely do — that's 70-80% of your insured salary for up to two years, depending on your contribution period. This is not a failure. This is the system working as designed for exactly this situation: a senior professional between engagements. Start the paperwork now, not when the reserves run out. The RAV (Regionales Arbeitsvermittlungszentrum) process takes time, and you'll need to register before your reserves are exhausted.

### 2. Pick ONE commercial lane and drive it hard for 90 days

Right now you're building proof-of-concept after proof-of-concept but converting nothing to revenue. The question isn't "can Miguel build impressive things?" — the answer is obviously yes. The question is "which of these impressive things can someone pay for in the next 90 days?"

Your most credible options, in my assessment:

**Option A: Consulting (fastest to cash).** You have BigBear Engineering GmbH already set up. You have 35 years of DACH industrial automation relationships. The pitch is simple: "I'll modernize your development workflow using agentic AI — here's the CRM I built in two afternoons as proof." Target: Komax alumni network, OPC UA community contacts, Swiss IoT companies. One CHF 15-30k consulting engagement buys you another 3-6 months of runway. This is the least glamorous option but the most likely to produce income before your reserves run out.

**Option B: Agentegra (medium-term, higher variance).** Go hard on Startup Days, but don't wait for the event. Use March and April to get at least one industrial prospect to say "yes, we'd pilot this." Even a handshake agreement changes the pitch from "we're looking for customers" to "we have pilot interest." Your LinkedIn newsletter is the right channel — but write about the compliance deadline specifically, not the technology. The message is: "August 2026 is coming, are you ready?" Manufacturing CTOs who read that and feel a knot in their stomach are your customers.

**Option C: Contracting/employment (parallel track).** Keep applying, but broaden beyond dream labs. Your profile — senior engineering leader who actually builds things with AI agents, with industrial automation domain expertise — is rare. Companies doing Industry 4.0, digital twins, manufacturing AI, or compliance tooling would value you. Swiss and German companies like Siemens, ABB, Bosch, or their tier-1 suppliers might have roles that aren't posted on public boards. Your network is your advantage here.

### 3. Put the creative projects on maintenance mode

This will hurt to hear, but the music videos, the story videos, and the social media content should be reduced to a sustainable trickle — not stopped, but not the focus. Post what you've already produced. Don't generate new batches. The book is written and submitted — let it ride. If the publisher says no, do the Kindle publish in a single focused weekend and move on.

These projects are beautiful and they demonstrate your creative range, but they're consuming production time that could be spent on client outreach, and they won't generate meaningful income in your 3-month window.

### 4. Use Robot Ross as a sales tool, not a product

Robot Ross is your best demo. It's visceral — people watch a robot draw on paper and they *get it*. But right now it's a product without a market. Reframe it: Robot Ross isn't the business. Robot Ross is the proof that your agentic infrastructure works. Every meeting, every pitch, every LinkedIn post should include: "Let me show you what my agent fleet built this week." That's the story. The Shopify store with 5 USDC drawings is charming but it's not a business model — it's a demo.

### 5. The Mexico trip is fine — but come back with a plan

Two weeks visiting family is not the problem. The problem is coming back and continuing to build in all directions simultaneously. Use the travel time to think, not to code. Come back with one commercial focus and a 90-day plan with specific revenue targets.
